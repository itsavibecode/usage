Privacy Policy Last updated 2026-06-12

Usage Tracker is a personal-use hobby app. This section explains, in plain English, what data the app collects, where it's stored, and who else touches it.

What the app stores about you

  • Google account info — when you sign in with Google, Firebase Auth records your Google account's unique ID, email address, display name, and profile picture URL. This is the standard sign-in payload from Google.
  • Products you enter — everything you type into the Add/Edit form: product name, type, size, cost (with and without tax), start/end dates, purchase date, store, buyer name, last-four of the payment card, UPC, brand, notes, bundle status, favorite rating and reason.
  • Activity log — an internal log of when you added, edited, duplicated, or deleted a product. Used to render the Activity tab.
  • Viewing preferences — pre-tax display, currency, density, which columns are visible, page size, last-seen changelog version.
  • Email-reminder settings — whether you've opted in, and (if provided) the email address to send reminders to.
  • UPC-lookup cache — when you scan or look up a UPC, the response is cached under your account so repeat lookups are instant and don't burn quota.

Where it lives

  • Firebase (Google Cloud) hosts the authentication service and Firestore database that stores all of the above. Firestore security rules restrict every document to its owner — no one else's account (including the developer's) can read your product list.
  • Your browser's localStorage holds a fast-access copy of your viewing preferences and the UPC cache so the app feels snappy offline. It's per-browser and cleared when you clear browser data.
  • Cloudflare Workers (behind the scenes) runs the daily reorder-reminder cron and — with a temporary Google service-account token — reads only the fields it needs to send that email.

Who else the app talks to

  • Google — sign-in (Firebase Auth), database (Firestore), and static hosting for the app itself (GitHub Pages, which is Microsoft-owned; the mirror is served from the same origin).
  • UPCitemdb and OpenFoodFacts — when you look up a UPC, the code is sent to one or both services to fetch product name / brand / image. The lookup is proxied through a Google Apps Script endpoint owned by the developer to avoid CORS, but no additional identifying data is added.
  • openFDA — the recall-alerts banner queries the FDA's public Enforcement Reports API by brand name (not UPC) to check for open recalls. Matching is brand-only and imprecise; see the Disclaimer.
  • logo.dev — displays brand logos next to products. The brand name is included in the image URL; nothing else about you is sent.
  • Resend — if you opt in to reorder-reminder emails, the daily cron uses Resend to deliver them. Only your email address and the reminder content are sent to Resend.

What the app doesn't collect

  • No analytics tracker. No Google Analytics, no Plausible, no anything.
  • No ad networks. No tracking pixels. No fingerprinting scripts.
  • No location data. The app doesn't ask for geolocation.
  • No camera images. The UPC scanner runs entirely in your browser; the video stream never leaves your device. Only the decoded barcode string is used.
  • No third-party session cookies. Only Firebase's own auth cookie for keeping you signed in.

Controlling and exporting your data

  • Export — the Export JSON / Export CSV buttons dump every product you've tracked into a file you can save.
  • Delete a product — the trash icon on any row deletes that single record.
  • Clear activity — the Clear button on the Activity tab wipes the log.
  • Delete your account — sign out and open a GitHub issue on the repo asking for account deletion. Alternatively, revoke the app's access from your Google Account's security settings; the Firestore data will still exist under your UID but you can request removal.
  • Contact — file an issue at github.com/itsavibecode/usage/issues. Because this is a personal project, there's no support inbox or SLA.

Children

Usage Tracker isn't directed at children under 13 and requires a Google account to sign in (which has its own age minimum). If a child has signed in and you're their guardian, contact the repo and the account will be deleted.

Changes

If the app starts collecting a new kind of data or talking to a new third party, this page will be updated and the "Last updated" date bumped. Material changes will also show up in the in-app "What's new" modal.

Disclaimer Last updated 2026-06-12

The rest of the page is legal-shaped. This section is the practical version — what the app's numbers actually mean, and what they don't.

TL;DR: Usage Tracker's estimates are averages built from what you've told it. Treat every number as a rough guide, not a certified measurement.

Reorder reminders are averages, not guarantees

The Reorder Reminders panel and the daily email digest flag active products when they've reached ~85% of the average lifespan for their product type. That average is calculated from your own past finished products (minimum two per type). The math is honest but the world is messy: your next tube of toothpaste might last twice as long or half as long as the last one. If a reminder fires and the current product still has plenty left, ignore it. If a reminder never fires and you run out, that's a data-not-enough problem, not a bug.

Cost math is only as good as your input

Per-day cost, per-unit cost, and total spend are derived from the size, cost, bundle, and dates you enter. Enter a wrong price and every dependent number is wrong. Bundle-mode expects the full bundle price, not the per-item price — the app labels the input and shows a live per-item derivation to make this hard to get wrong, but you can still fool it.

FDA recall alerts are brand-only matched

The recall banner queries the openFDA Enforcement Reports API by brand name only. It doesn't check UPC. If a recall covers only one flavor / size / lot of a brand you own, this app will flag every product of that brand — including ones that are not affected. It's a first-look nudge, not a definitive answer.

Always verify a recall on the FDA's own site before assuming a specific bottle in your bathroom is affected. Each recall card includes a link to the official FDA record. Use it.

Conversely, the FDA API only covers FDA-regulated categories (mostly drugs, food, medical devices). Products outside that scope (small kitchen appliances, apparel) aren't checked at all. Absence of a recall banner is not evidence that a product is safe.

Not medical advice

Usage Tracker treats over-the-counter medication like any other consumable — a product with a start date, size, and cost. It does not know whether a medication is safe for you, whether an expiration date has passed, or whether a recall applies to your specific lot. Follow the manufacturer's instructions and consult a pharmacist or physician for anything medication-related.

Not financial advice

The cost dashboards exist to help you notice spending patterns on household goods. They are not a substitute for budgeting software, a professional accountant, or a financial advisor.

UPC-lookup data is third-party

When the app fills in a product name, brand, or image from a UPC, that information came from UPCitemdb or OpenFoodFacts — public databases populated by many contributors. It can be wrong, outdated, or missing. Always verify a match before saving.

Logos and product images shown in the app come from third-party services (logo.dev for brand logos; UPCitemdb / OpenFoodFacts for product images) and remain the trademarks and copyrights of their respective owners. Their appearance in the app does not imply endorsement of Usage Tracker by any brand.
